2.2 Quantum channels
--------------------

Quantum channels represent discrete changes in states of registers that are to be considered physically realizable (in an idealized sense). For example, the steps of a quantum computation, or any other processing of quantum information, as well as the effects of errors and noise on quantum registers, are modeled as quantum channels.

### 2.2.1 Definitions and basic notions concerning channels

In mathematical terms, a quantum channel is a linear map, from one space of square operators to another, that satisfies the two conditions of complete positivity and trace preservation.

> **Definition 2.13** A quantum channel (or simply a **channel**, for short) is a linear map
>
> $$
> \Phi :L\left(X\right)→L\left(Y\right)
> $$
>
> (i.e., an element  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)\left[cite_{s}tart\right]$ ), for some choice of complex Euclidean spaces  $X$  and  $Y$ , satisfying two properties: 1\.  $\Phi$  is completely positive. 2\.  $\Phi$  is trace preserving.

The collection of all channels of the form (2.49) is denoted  $C\left(X,Y\right)$ , and one writes  $C\left(X\right)$  as a shorthand for  $C\left(X,X\right)$ . For a given choice of registers  $X$  and  $Y$ , one may view that a channel of the form  $\Phi \in C\left(X,Y\right)$  is a transformation from  $X$  into  $Y$ . That is, when such a transformation takes place, it is to be viewed that the register  $X$  ceases to exist, with  $Y$  being formed in its place. Moreover, the state of  $Y$  is obtained by applying the map  $\Phi$  to the state  $\rho \in D\left(X\right)$  of  $X$ , yielding  $\Phi \left(\rho \right)\in D\left(Y\right)$ . When it is the case that  $X=Y$  one may simply view that the state of the register  $X$  has been changed according to the mapping  $\Phi$ .

> **Example 2.14** Let  $X$  be a complex Euclidean space and let  $U\in U\left(X\right)$  be a unitary operator. The map  $\Phi \in C\left(X\right)$  defined by
>
> $$
> \Phi \left(X\right)=UXU^{\ast }
> $$
>
> for every  $X\in L\left(X\right)$  is an example of a channel. Channels of this form are called **unitary channels**. The identity channel  $1_{L\left(X\right)}$  is one example of a unitary channel, obtained by setting  $U=1_{X}$ . Intuitively speaking, this channel represents an ideal quantum communication channel or a perfect component in a quantum computer memory, which causes no change in the state of the register  $X$  it acts upon.

> **Example 2.15** Let  $X$  and  $Y$  be complex Euclidean spaces, and let  $\sigma \in D\left(Y\right)$  be a density operator. The mapping  $\Phi \in C\left(X,Y\right)$  defined by
>
> $$
> \Phi \left(X\right)=Tr\left(X\right)\sigma ,
> $$
>
> for every  $X\in L\left(X\right)$ , is a channel. It holds that  $\Phi \left(\rho \right)=\sigma$  for every  $\rho \in D\left(X\right)$ ; in effect, the channel represents the action of discarding the register  $X$ , and replacing it with the register  $Y$  initialized to the state  $\sigma$ . Channels of this form will be called **replacement channels**.

#### Product channels

Suppose  $X_{1},...,X_{n}$  and  $Y_{1},...,Y_{n}$  are registers, and recall that one denotes by  $X_{1},...,X_{n}$  and  $Y_{1},...,Y_{n}$  the complex Euclidean spaces associated with these registers. A channel

$$
\Phi \in C\left(X_{1}\otimes \cdot \cdot \cdot \otimes X_{n},Y_{1}\otimes \cdot \cdot \cdot \otimes Y_{n}\right)
$$

transforming  $\left(X_{1},...,X_{n}\right)$  into  $\left(Y_{1},...,Y_{n}\right)$  is called a **product channel** if

$$
\Phi =\Psi _{1}\otimes ...\otimes \Psi _{n}
$$

for some choice of channels  $\Psi _{1}\in C\left(X_{1},Y_{1}\right),...,\Psi _{n}\in C\left(X_{n},Y_{n}\right)$ . Product channels represent an independent application of a sequence of channels to a sequence of registers, in a similar way to product states representing independence among the states of registers.

An important special case involving independent channels is the situation in which a given channel is performed on one register, while nothing at all is done to one or more other registers under consideration. (As suggested in Example 2.14, the act of doing nothing at all to a register is equivalent to performing the identity channel on that register.)

> **Example 2.16** Suppose that  $X$ ,  $Y$ , and  $Z$  are registers, and  $\Phi \in C\left(X,Y\right)$  is a channel that transforms  $X$  into  $Y$ . Also suppose that the compound register  $\left(X,Z\right)$  is in some particular state  $\rho \in D\left(X\otimes Z\right)$  at some instant, and the channel  $\Phi$  is applied to  $X$ , transforming it into  $Y$ . The resulting state of the pair  $\left(Y,Z\right)$  is then given by
>
> $$
> \left(\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(Z\right)}\right)\left(\rho \right)\in D\left(Y\otimes Z\right),
> $$
>
> as one views that the identity channel  $1_{L\left(Z\right)}$  has independently been applied to the register  $Z$ .

Example 2.16 illustrates the importance of the requirement that channels are completely positive. That is, it must hold that  $\left(\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(Z\right)}\right)\left(\rho \right)$  is a density operator for every choice of  $Z$  and every density operator  $\rho \in D\left(X\otimes Z\right)$ , which together with the linearity of  $\Phi$  implies that  $\Phi$  is completely positive (in addition to being trace preserving).

#### State preparations as quantum channels

As stated in Section 2.1.1, a register is trivial if its classical state set consists of a single element. The complex Euclidean space associated with a trivial register is therefore one-dimensional: it must take the form  $C^{\left{a\right}}$  for  $\left{a\right}$  being the singleton classical state set of the register. No generality is lost in associating such a space with the field of complex numbers  $C$ , and in making the identification  $L\left(C\right)=C$ , one finds that the scalar 1 is the only possible state for a trivial register. As is to be expected, such a register is therefore completely useless from an information-processing viewpoint; the presence of a trivial register does nothing more than to tensor the scalar 1 to the state of any other registers under consideration.

It is instructive nevertheless to consider the properties of channels that involve trivial registers. Suppose, in particular, that  $X$  is a trivial register and  $Y$  is arbitrary, and consider a channel of the form  $\Phi \in C\left(X,Y\right)$  that transforms  $X$  into  $Y$ . It must hold that  $\Phi$  is given by

$$
\Phi \left(\alpha \right)=\alpha \rho
$$

for all  $\alpha \in C$ , for some choice of  $\rho \in D\left(Y\right)$ , as  $\Phi$  must be linear and it must hold that  $\Phi \left(1\right)$  is positive semidefinite and has trace equal to one. The channel  $\Phi$  defined by (2.55) may be viewed as the preparation of the quantum state  $\rho$  in a new register  $Y$ . The trivial register  $X$  can be considered as being essentially a placeholder for this preparation, which is to occur at whatever moment the channel is performed. In this way, a state preparation may be seen as the application of this form of channel.

To see that every mapping of the form (2.55) is indeed a channel, for an arbitrary choice of a density operator  $\rho \in D\left(Y\right)$ , one may check that the conditions of complete positivity and trace preservation hold. The mapping  $\Phi$  given by (2.55) is obviously trace preserving whenever  $Tr\left(\rho \right)=1$ , and the complete positivity of  $\Phi$  is implied by the following simple proposition.

> **Proposition 2.17** Let  $Y$  be a complex Euclidean space and let  $P\in Pos\left(Y\right)$  be a positive semidefinite operator. The mapping  $\Phi \in T\left(C,Y\right)$  defined as  $\Phi \left(\alpha \right)=\alpha P$  for all  $\alpha \in C$  is completely positive.

> **Proof** Let  $Z$  be any complex Euclidean space. The action of the mapping  $\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(Z\right)}$  on an operator  $Z\in L\left(Z\right)=L\left(C\otimes Z\right)$  is given by
>
> $$
> \left(\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(Z\right)}\right)\left(Z\right)=P\otimes Z
> $$
>
> If  $Z$  is positive semidefinite, then  $P\otimes Z$  is positive semidefinite as well, and therefore  $\Phi$  is completely positive.

#### The trace mapping as a channel

Another situation in which a channel involves a trivial register is when this channel transforms an arbitrary register  $X$  into a trivial register  $Y$ . By identifying the complex Euclidean space  $Y$  with the complex numbers  $C$  as before, one has that the channel  $\Phi$  must take the form  $\Phi \in C\left(X,C\right)$ . The only mapping of this form that can possibly preserve trace is the trace mapping itself, and so it must hold that

$$
\Phi \left(X\right)=Tr\left(X\right)
$$

for all  $X\in L\left(X\right)$ . To say that a register  $X$  has been transformed into a trivial register  $Y$  is tantamount to saying that  $X$  has been destroyed, discarded, or simply ignored. This channel was, in effect, introduced in Section 2.1.3 when reductions of quantum states were defined.

In order to conclude that the trace mapping is indeed a valid channel, it is necessary to verify that it is completely positive. One way to prove this simple fact is to combine the following proposition with Proposition 2.17.

> **Proposition 2.18** Let  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$  be a positive map, for  $X$  and  $Y$  being complex Euclidean spaces. It holds that  $\Phi ^{\ast }$  is positive.

> **Proof** By the positivity of  $\Phi$ , it holds that  $\Phi \left(P\right)\in Pos\left(Y\right)$  for every positive semidefinite operator  $P\in Pos\left(X\right)$ , which is equivalent to the condition that
>
> $$
> \left⟨Q,\Phi \left(P\right)\right⟩\ge 0
> $$
>
> for all  $P\in Pos\left(X\right)$  and  $Q\in Pos\left(Y\right)$ . It follows that
>
> $$
> \left⟨\Phi ^{\ast }\left(Q\right),P\right⟩=\left⟨Q,\Phi \left(P\right)\right⟩\ge 0
> $$
>
> for all  $P\in Pos\left(X\right)$  and  $Q\in Pos\left(Y\right)$ , which is equivalent to  $\Phi ^{\ast }\left(Q\right)\in Pos\left(X\right)$  for every  $Q\in Pos\left(Y\right)$ . The mapping  $\Phi ^{\ast }$  is therefore positive.

**Remark** Proposition 2.18 implies that if  $\Phi \in CP\left(X,Y\right)$  is a completely positive map, then the adjoint map  $\Phi ^{\ast }$  is also completely positive; for if  $\Phi$  is completely positive, then  $\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(Z\right)}$  is positive for every complex Euclidean space  $Z$ , and therefore  $\left(\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(Z\right)}\right)^{\ast }=\Phi ^{\ast }\otimes 1_{L\left(Z\right)}$  is also positive.

> **Corollary 2.19** The trace mapping  $Tr\in T\left(X,C\right)$ , for any choice of a complex Euclidean space  $X$ , is completely positive.

> **Proof** The adjoint of the trace is given by  $Tr^{\ast }\left(\alpha \right)=\alpha 1_{X}$  for every  $\alpha \in C$ . This map is completely positive by Proposition 2.17, therefore the trace map is completely positive by the remark above.

### 2.2.2 Representations and characterizations of channels

Suppose  $\Phi \in C\left(X,Y\right)$  is a channel, for  $X$  and  $Y$  being complex Euclidean spaces. It may, in some situations, be sufficient to view such a channel abstractly, as a completely positive and trace-preserving linear map of the form  $\Phi :L\left(X\right)→L\left(Y\right)$  and nothing more. In other situations, it may be useful to consider a more concrete representation of such a channel. Four specific representations of channels (and of arbitrary maps of the form  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$ , for complex Euclidean spaces  $X$  and  $Y$ ) are discussed in this section.

#### The natural representation

For any choice of complex Euclidean spaces  $X$  and  $Y$ , and for every linear map  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$ , it is evident that the mapping

$$
vec\left(X\right)↦vec\left(\Phi \left(X\right)\right)
$$

is linear, as it can be represented as a composition of linear mappings. There must therefore exist a linear operator  $K\left(\Phi \right)\in L\left(X\otimes X,Y\otimes Y\right)$  for which it holds that

$$
K\left(\Phi \right)vec\left(X\right)=vec\left(\Phi \left(X\right)\right)
$$

for all  $X\in L\left(X\right)$ . The operator  $K\left(\Phi \right)$ , which is uniquely determined by the requirement that (2.61) holds for all  $X\in L\left(X\right)$ , is the **natural representation** of  $\Phi$ , as it directly represents the action of  $\Phi$  as a linear map (with respect to the operator-vector correspondence). It may be noted that the mapping  $K:T\left(X,Y\right)→L\left(X\otimes X,Y\otimes Y\right)$  is linear:

$$
K\left(\alpha \Phi +\beta \Psi \right)=\alpha K\left(\Phi \right)+\beta K\left(\Psi \right)
$$

for all choices of  $\alpha ,\beta \in C$  and  $\Phi ,\Psi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$ . Moreover,  $K$  is a bijection, as the action of a given mapping  $\Phi$  can be recovered from  $K\left(\Phi \right)$ ; for each operator  $X\in L\left(X\right)$ , one has that  $Y=\Phi \left(X\right)$  is the unique operator satisfying  $vec\left(Y\right)=K\left(\Phi \right)vec\left(X\right)$ . The natural representation respects the notion of adjoints, meaning that

$$
K\left(\Phi ^{\ast }\right)=\left(K\left(\Phi \right)\right)^{\ast }
$$

for every map  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$ .

#### The Choi representation

For any choice of complex Euclidean spaces  $X$  and  $Y$ , one may define a mapping  $J:T\left(X,Y\right)→L\left(Y\otimes X\right)$  as

$$
J\left(\Phi \right)=\left(\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(X\right)}\right)\left(vec\left(1_{X}\right)vec\left(1_{X}\right)^{\ast }\right)
$$

for each  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$ . Alternatively, under the assumption that  $X=C^{\Sigma }$ , one may write

$$
J\left(\Phi \right)=\sum_{a,b\in \Sigma } \Phi \left(E_{a,b}\right)\otimes E_{a,b}
$$

The operator  $J\left(\Phi \right)$  is called the **Choi representation** (or the **Choi operator**) of  $\Phi$ . It is evident from the equation (2.65) that the mapping  $J$  is a linear bijection. An alternative way to prove that the mapping  $J$  is a bijection is to observe that the action of the mapping  $\Phi$  can be recovered from the operator  $J\left(\Phi \right)$  by means of the equation

$$
\Phi \left(X\right)=Tr_{X}\left(J\left(\Phi \right)\left(1_{Y}\otimes X^{T}\right)\right)
$$

. A given map  $\Phi$  is completely positive if and only if  $J\left(\Phi \right)$  is positive semidefinite. For a given map  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$ , the rank of its Choi representation  $J\left(\Phi \right)$  is called the **Choi rank** of  $\Phi$ .

#### Kraus representations

For any choice of complex Euclidean spaces  $X$  and  $Y$ , an alphabet  $\Sigma$ , and collections  $\left{A_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}$  and  $\left{B_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}$  of operators drawn from the space  $L\left(X,Y\right)$ , one may define a linear map  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$  as

$$
\Phi \left(X\right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a}XB_{a\ast }
$$

for every  $X\in L\left(X\right)$ . The expression (2.68) is a **Kraus representation** of the map  $\Phi$ . Kraus representations exist for every map of the form  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$ . Unlike the natural representation and Choi representation, however, Kraus representations are not unique. Under the assumption that  $\Phi$  is determined by the above equation (2.68), it holds that

$$
\Phi ^{\ast }\left(Y\right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a\ast }YB_{a},
$$

as follows from a calculation relying on the cyclic property of the trace. It is established by Theorem 2.22 below, such representations with  $A_{a}=B_{a}$  for each  $a\in \Sigma$  exist precisely when the map being considered is completely positive.

#### Stinespring representations

Suppose  $X,Y,$  and  $Z$  are complex Euclidean spaces and  $A,B\in L\left(X,Y\otimes Z\right)$  are operators. One may then define a map  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$  as

$$
\Phi \left(X\right)=Tr_{Z}\left(AXB^{\ast }\right)
$$

for every  $X\in L\left(X\right)$ . The expression (2.71) is a **Stinespring representation** of the map  $\Phi$ . Similar to Kraus representations, Stinespring representations always exist for a given map  $\Phi$ , and are not unique. If a map  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$  has a Stinespring representation taking the form (2.71), then it holds that

$$
\Phi ^{\ast }\left(Y\right)=A^{\ast }\left(Y\otimes 1_{Z}\right)B
$$

for all  $Y\in L\left(Y\right)$ . Such representations with  $A=B$  exist if and only if  $\Phi$  is completely positive.

#### Relationships among the representations

> **Proposition 2.20** Let  $X$  and  $Y$  be complex Euclidean spaces, let  $\Sigma$  be an alphabet, let  $\left{A_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}$ ,  $\left{B_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}\subset L\left(X,Y\right)$  be collections of operators indexed by  $\Sigma$ , and let  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$ . The following four statements are equivalent: 1\. (Natural representation.)  $K\left(\Phi \right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a}\otimes B_{a}​.$ 2\. (Choi representation.)  $J\left(\Phi \right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } vec\left(A_{a}\right)vec\left(B_{a}\right)^{\ast }.$ 3\. (Kraus representations.)  $\Phi \left(X\right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a}XB_{a\ast }$  for all  $X\in L\left(X\right).$ 4\. (Stinespring representations.) For  $Z=C^{\Sigma }$  and  $A,B\in L\left(X,Y\otimes Z\right)$  defined as  $A=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a}\otimes e_{a}$  and  $B=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } B_{a}\otimes e_{a},$  it holds that  $\Phi \left(X\right)=Tr_{Z}\left(AXB^{\ast }\right)$  for all  $X\in L\left(X\right).$

> **Proof** The equivalence between statements 3 and 4 is a straightforward calculation. The equivalence between statements 1 and 3 follows from the identity  $vec\left(A_{a}XB_{a\ast }\right)=\left(A_{a}\otimes B_{a}​\right)vec\left(X\right)$ . Finally, the equivalence between statements 2 and 3 follows from the equations  $\left(A_{a}\otimes 1_{X}\right)vec\left(1_{X}\right)=vec\left(A_{a}\right)$  and  $vec\left(1_{X}\right)^{\ast }\left(B_{a\ast }\otimes 1_{X}\right)=vec\left(B_{a}\right)^{\ast }$ .

> **Corollary 2.21** Let  $X$  and  $Y$  be complex Euclidean spaces, let  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$  be a nonzero linear map, and let  $r=rank\left(J\left(\Phi \right)\right)$  be the Choi rank of  $\Phi$ . The following two facts hold: 1\. For  $\Sigma$  being any alphabet with  $∣\Sigma ∣=r$ , there exists a Kraus representation of  $\Phi$  having the form  $\Phi \left(X\right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a}XB_{a\ast }$  for some choice of  $\left{A_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}$ ,  $\left{B_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}\subset L\left(X,Y\right)$ . 2\. For  $Z$  being any complex Euclidean space with  $dim\left(Z\right)=r$ , there exists a Stinespring representation of  $\Phi$  having the form  $\Phi \left(X\right)=Tr_{Z}\left(AXB^{\ast }\right)$ , for some choice of operators  $A,B\in L\left(X,Y\otimes Z\right)$ .

> **Proof** For  $\Sigma$  being any alphabet with  $∣\Sigma ∣=r$ , it is possible to write  $J\left(\Phi \right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } u_{a}v_{a\ast }$  for some choice of vectors  $\left{u_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right},\left{v_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}\subset Y\otimes X$ . One may take  $\left{u_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}$  to be any basis for the image of  $J\left(\Phi \right)$ , which uniquely determines  $\left{v_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}$ . Taking  $vec\left(A_{a}\right)=u_{a}$  and  $vec\left(B_{a}\right)=v_{a}$ , it follows from Proposition 2.20 that the expression is a Kraus representation of  $\Phi$ . Moreover, it holds that the expression (2.82) is a Stinespring representation of  $\Phi$  for  $A,B\in L\left(X,Y\otimes Z\right)$  defined as  $A=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a}\otimes e_{a}$  and  $B=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } B_{a}\otimes e_{a}$ .

#### Characterizations of completely positive maps

> **Theorem 2.22** Let  $\Phi \in T\left(X,Y\right)$  be a nonzero map, for complex Euclidean spaces  $X$  and  $Y$ . The following statements are equivalent: 1\.  $\Phi$  is completely positive. 2\.  $\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(X\right)}$  is positive. 3\.  $J\left(\Phi \right)\in Pos\left(Y\otimes X\right)$ 4\. There exists a collection  $\left{A_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}\subset L\left(X,Y\right)$  for which  $\Phi \left(X\right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a}XA_{a\ast }$  for all  $X\in L\left(X\right).$ 5\. Statement 4 holds for an alphabet  $\Sigma$  satisfying  $∣\Sigma ∣=rank\left(J\left(\Phi \right)\right).$ 6\. There exists an operator  $A\in L\left(X,Y\otimes Z\right)$  such that  $\Phi \left(X\right)=Tr_{Z}\left(AXA^{\ast }\right)$  for all  $X\in L\left(X\right).$ 7\. Statement 6 holds for  $Z$  having dimension equal to  $rank\left(J\left(\Phi \right)\right).$

> **Proof** The theorem will be proved by establishing  $\left(1\right)⇒\left(2\right)⇒\left(3\right)⇒\left(5\right)⇒\left(4\right)⇒\left(1\right)$  and  $\left(5\right)⇒\left(7\right)⇒\left(6\right)⇒\left(1\right)$ . Some implications are immediate: (1) implies (2) by definition, (5) trivially implies (4), (7) trivially implies (6), and (5) implies (7) by Proposition 2.20. Assume (2). Because  $vec\left(1_{X}\right)vec\left(1_{X}\right)^{\ast }\in Pos\left(X\otimes X\right)$  and  $J\left(\Phi \right)=\left(\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(X\right)}\right)\left(vec\left(1_{X}\right)vec\left(1_{X}\right)^{\ast }\right)$ , it follows  $J\left(\Phi \right)\in Pos\left(Y\otimes X\right)$ , so (2) implies (3). Assume (3). By the spectral theorem, one may write  $J\left(\Phi \right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } u_{a}u_{a\ast }$  for some choice of alphabet  $\Sigma$  with  $∣\Sigma ∣=rank\left(J\left(\Phi \right)\right)$ . Taking  $vec\left(A_{a}\right)=u_{a}$ , one has  $J\left(\Phi \right)=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } vec\left(A_{a}\right)vec\left(A_{a}\right)^{\ast }$ . Equation (2.87) holds by Proposition 2.20, establishing that (3) implies (5). Suppose (4). For a positive semidefinite operator  $P\in Pos\left(X\otimes W\right)$ , it is evident  $\left(A_{a}\otimes 1_{W}\right)P\left(A_{a}\otimes 1_{W}\right)^{\ast }\in Pos\left(Y\otimes W\right)$ , and therefore  $\left(\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(W\right)}\right)\left(P\right)\in Pos\left(Y\otimes W\right)$  by the fact that  $Pos\left(Y\otimes W\right)$  is a convex cone. Thus  $\Phi$  is completely positive, so (4) implies (1). Finally, suppose (6). For any positive semidefinite operator  $P\in Pos\left(X\otimes W\right)$ ,  $\left(A\otimes 1_{W}\right)P\left(A\otimes 1_{W}\right)^{\ast }\in Pos\left(Y\otimes Z\otimes W\right)$ , so that  $\left(\Phi \otimes 1_{L\left(W\right)}\right)\left(P\right)=Tr_{Z}\left(\left(A\otimes 1_{W}\right)P\left(A\otimes 1_{W}\right)^{\ast }\right)\in Pos\left(Y\otimes W\right)$  by the complete positivity of the trace (Corollary 2.19). Thus (6) implies (1).

> **Corollary 2.23** Let  $X$  and  $Y$  be complex Euclidean spaces, and assume  $\left{A_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}$ ,  $\left{B_{a}:a\in \Sigma \right}\subset L\left(X,Y\right)$  satisfy  $\sum_{a\in \Sigma } A_{a}XA_{a\ast }=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } B_{a}XB_{a\ast }$  for all  $X\in L\left(X\right)$ . There exists a unitary operator  $U\in U\left(C^{\Sigma }\right)$  such that  $B_{a}=\sum_{b\in \Sigma } U\left(a,b\right)A_{b}$  for all  $a\in \Sigma$ .

> **Proof** The maps agree for all  $X$ , so their Choi representations must be equal:  $\sum_{a\in \Sigma } vec\left(A_{a}\right)vec\left(A_{a}\right)^{\ast }=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } vec\left(B_{a}\right)vec\left(B_{a}\right)^{\ast }$ . Let  $Z=C^{\Sigma }$  and define  $u=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } vec\left(A_{a}\right)\otimes e_{a}$  and  $v=\sum_{a\in \Sigma } vec\left(B_{a}\right)\otimes e_{a}$  so that  $Tr_{Z}\left(uu^{\ast }\right)=Tr_{Z}\left(vv^{\ast }\right)$ .
